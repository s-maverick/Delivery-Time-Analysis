Abstract: In the fast-paced domain of e-commerce grocery delivery, minimizing delivery time is critical to ensuring customer satisfaction and sustaining operational efficiency. This study investigates the multifactorial influences on delivery delays across major platforms—Blinkit, Swiggy Instamart, and JioMart—by analyzing large-scale transactional data. Specifically, it examines how static factors like order value and platform type, alongside dynamic environmental variables such as real-time traffic density and weather conditions, affect delivery performance. Using multiple linear regression models and interaction terms, the analysis identifies significant predictors of delay and highlights context-specific operational challenges. The findings provide actionable insights for optimizing last-mile logistics under varying real-world conditions.

1. Introduction

The rapid growth of e-commerce grocery services has transformed last-mile delivery into a cornerstone of platform competitiveness. As customer expectations for speed and reliability rise, understanding the underlying factors that influence delivery times becomes increasingly vital. While prior studies have addressed platform efficiency and customer satisfaction in general, few have systematically examined how delivery time is shaped by a combination of operational metrics and external disruptions. This research explores two central questions: (1) how platform-specific attributes and order value contribute to delivery speed, and (2) whether dynamic conditions like road traffic density and weather anomalies have a measurable effect on delivery delays. By leveraging real-world datasets and regression modeling, this study aims to uncover insights that can inform intelligent route planning, dynamic resource allocation, and accurate delivery time forecasting in urban logistics.

2. Research Questions

1. How do various factors such as order value, platform impact grocery delivery times in e-commerce platforms (Blinkit, Swiggy Instamart, JioMart)?

Lets say you are placing an urgent grocery order late in the evening, expecting a quick delivery for an important family dinner. However, due to a surge in discount-driven orders and an unusually high volume of cash-on-delivery payments, the delivery arrives much later than anticipated, causing frustration. Such incidents highlight the complexity of delivery logistics and the various factors that influence delivery time. Delivery time is a critical metric for customer satisfaction and operational efficiency. A seamless, timely delivery experience not only enhances brand loyalty but also ensures smooth logistics management. Platforms often drive demand and order volume, but if a platform experiences a surge in orders without scaling up its resources, it might lead to operational bottlenecks, affecting delivery efficiency. During peak usage periods, overwhelmed delivery personnel, stock shortages, and increased traffic congestion could contribute to significant delays. Understanding these dynamics helps e-commerce platforms optimize their operations without compromising service speed. Or maybe these are just strategies to attract more customers. Additionally, order date and time play a crucial role—weekend orders may experience longer delivery times due to higher demand, whereas weekday orders might be processed faster with smoother logistics. High-value orders, often requiring extra verification or special handling, can further impact delivery times, making it essential for platforms to streamline logistics for premium customers. This study fills that gap by analyzing real-world data,the role of payment methods and discount strategies in delivery efficiency remains underexplored.

2. Does real-time traffic density and weather conditions have a measurable impact on delivery delays ?

Imagine you’ve just placed an order for essentials during the monsoon season, expecting them to arrive before a storm hits. However, the delivery ends up being delayed by hours. Later, you learn that heavy rainfall had flooded local roads, causing severe traffic jams and forcing delivery riders to reroute through longer, slower paths. Or perhaps your delivery was scheduled during evening rush hour, when urban congestion hits its peak — again, pushing back your expected delivery time. These real-life inconveniences underscore how unpredictable and uncontrollable external conditions like traffic density and weather can severely impact delivery timelines. Delivery time isn’t just a logistical metric; it’s a key indicator of customer satisfaction and service reliability. A delay caused by rain or a traffic jam might be seen as excusable once — but repeated experiences can damage customer trust and loyalty. For e-commerce grocery platforms like Blinkit, Swiggy Instamart, and JioMart, managing deliveries efficiently under varying real-time conditions is a complex challenge. Rush hours, weekend congestion, or weather anomalies such as fog or heatwaves add layers of unpredictability to already intricate delivery networks. Despite advancements in real-time tracking and route optimization, many platforms still don’t systematically integrate real-time traffic and weather data into their delivery prediction models. This oversight often leads to inaccuracies in promised delivery times and unfulfilled customer expectations. This study aims to bridge that gap by examining whether these dynamic, environmental factors — traffic density and weather — have a statistically measurable impact on delivery delays.

3. Hypothesis

Existing Research and Gaps

Several studies have explored factors influencing delivery performance in e-commerce and grocery delivery services. However, existing literature lacks an in-depth comparative analysis of multiple platforms (Blinkit, Swiggy Instamart, and JioMart) and their unique operational factors. Additionally, while studies have examined the impact of delivery distance and order value on delivery time, they have not comprehensively assessed how payment methods and discount-driven demand surges influence delays in the context of online grocery delivery.
Moreover, research such as Chopra et al. (2020) explored consumer behavior in online grocery retailing, but did not analyze how operational variations between platforms impact efficiency. Also, the role of payment methods (e.g., COD vs. digital) in causing delays remains underexplored in grocery logistics, despite COD being a common cause of inefficiencies in traditional retail logistics (as highlighted in Patel et al., 2023). The relationship between discount incentives and delivery performance is well-documented in retail (Jha & Kumar, 2023), but its effect on last-mile grocery logistics and time frame or order remains largely untested.

Hypotheses to be Tested

H1: Order value influence delivery time, with high value orders experiencing longer delays compared to low value.

H2: Real-time traffic density and weather conditions significantly impact food delivery time.

The linear regression model examines the relationship between delivery time (in minutes) and order value (in INR). The intercept of the model is approximately 29.43, indicating that the average delivery time, even when the order value is ₹0 (which is impossible technically, but we can consider this as baseline time), is around 29.43 minutes. The coefficient for order value is 0.0001724, suggesting that for every ₹1 increase in order value, the delivery time increases by roughly 0.00017 minutes, or about one second per ₹60 spent. Although the p-value associated with the order value is 0.0223, indicating that the relationship is statistically significant at the 5% level, the practical significance of this finding is minimal. The increase in delivery time associated with order value is extremely small and unlikely to have a meaningful real-world impact. Furthermore, the R-squared value is only 0.0052%, which means that order value explains virtually none of the variation in delivery time. In conclusion, while there is a statistically significant association between order value and delivery time, the effect is so small that it is not practically useful for predicting delivery durations. This suggests that other factors, such as traffic conditions, distance, platform efficiency, and weather, may be far more influential in determining how long a delivery takes.

Here, the null hypothesis (H₀) is: There is no relationship between order value and delivery time.

Mathematically, this means: H₀: β₁ = 0

Where β₁ is the coefficient of the predictor variable Order.Value..INR..
This hypothesis suggests that changes in the order value have no effect on the delivery time. Since the p-value for Order.Value..INR. is 0.0223, which is less than 0.05, we reject the null hypothesis at the 5% significance level. This means there is statistical evidence of a (very weak) relationship between order value and delivery time.
